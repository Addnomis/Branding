# On Finishing Things

A reflection for the new year.

---

I built a personal finance dashboard this year. Not because I needed another project, but because I wanted to understand where my money was going. What I didn't expect was what the data would show me about myself.

The charts didn't just reveal spending patterns. They revealed *life* patterns.

## The Insight

I can sprint, but I can't jog.

Every graph showed the same shape: bursts of intensity followed by silence. Savings contributions. Project commits. Time invested. All spiky. All inconsistent. The only flat line? Rent. Because it's automatic. Because I don't have to decide.

And that's when it clicked: **I leak energy in decisions.**

I can spend hours analyzing options, optimizing choices, weighing tradeoffs. It feels productive. It feels like progress. But it's not. It's just expensive thinking.

The things that actually work in my life are the ones that don't require me to show up motivated. They just happen.

## The Deeper Problem

But automation only explains part of it. There's something else.

I have a 3D model I've been working on for two years. A meditation app sitting at 95% complete. Ideas that never shipped. And for a long time, I told myself I was being a perfectionist. That I just needed higher standards.

That's not it.

**I don't want them to end.**

The project isn't the goal. The project is the excuse to keep playing in that creative space. The thinking phase is where the adventure lives - where imagination runs free, unconstrained by reality. Every possibility exists simultaneously.

But shipping? That collapses the wave function. The infinite becomes finite. The "could be" becomes "is." And that feels like loss.

## What I'm Actually Protecting

When I hold onto a project past its natural end, I'm protecting:

- The identity of "person working on this thing"
- The permission to spend time in that creative zone
- The open-ended possibility of what it could still become

And underneath all of that is a quiet fear: *What do I do after?*

If the app ships, I'm not "building the app" anymore. That chapter closes. And there's grief in that.

## The Reframe

Imagination without execution is just entertainment. It feels like adventure, but you're not actually going anywhere. You're on a treadmill in a very nice VR headset.

Real adventure has stakes. You can fail. It can be embarrassing. Users might not care. The vision in your head might not survive contact with reality.

But that's not constraint - that's *feedback*. And feedback is what makes the next adventure actually different from the last one.

**Holding on past the natural end is taxidermy.** You're preserving the shape of the adventure without any of the life in it.

## For the New Year

Ship it. Grieve it. See what wants to come next.

Your imagination doesn't die when you finish something. It just gets a new problem to play with.

The blank canvas is waiting.

---

*Written on the last days of 2025, while staring at a dashboard full of my own patterns.*
